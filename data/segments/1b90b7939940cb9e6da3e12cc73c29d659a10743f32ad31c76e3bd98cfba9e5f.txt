Claims:
- [fact] O'Reilly and Tushman restate James March's 1991 distinction: exploitation is about efficiency, control, certainty, and variance reduction, while exploration is about search, discovery, autonomy, and innovation. Source: O'Reilly and Tushman 2013 working paper, discussion of March (1991).
- [fact] Lean Startup guidance says teams should widen distribution only after a minimum viable product (MVP) has generated actionable measurement, established customers, and clarified whether to pivot or persevere. Source: Eric Ries, "Methodology," sections "Work Smarter not Harder," "Develop An MVP," and "Build-Measure-Learn" - https://theleanstartup.com/principles
- [inference] The transition boundary is therefore not "the demo worked once"; it is the point at which uncertainty has been reduced enough to specify where the capability works, where it fails, what it costs, and what operating pattern should be repeated. Basis: March distinction plus Lean Startup learning gate logic.
- [fact] The prior exploit-explore portfolio item found that exploration and exploitation require differentiated governance, because applying exploitation gates too early suppresses learning while applying exploration logic too long prevents value capture. Source: `Research/completed/2026-02-28-exploit-explore-ai-portfolio-framework.md`, Findings and Analysis.
- [inference] A transition exists even when the organisation does not name it explicitly, because governance logic must change between learning under uncertainty and repeatable value capture. Basis: March framing, prior portfolio item, and Lean Startup gate logic.
