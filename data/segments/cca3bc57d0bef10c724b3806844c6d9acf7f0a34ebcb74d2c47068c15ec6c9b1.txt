- [fact; source: https://www.nber.org/system/files/chapters/c2144/c2144.pdf] Arrow states that information has a fundamental demand paradox: its value for the purchaser is not known until the purchaser has the information, but then the purchaser has in effect acquired it without cost.
- [inference; source: https://www.nber.org/system/files/chapters/c2144/c2144.pdf] Expert knowledge therefore creates an ex ante information asymmetry for the seeker, because the seeker needs some disclosure, demonstration, or expert interpretation to judge value, but that very process begins to transfer the knowledge and so raises verification cost for both sides.
- [inference; source: https://www.nber.org/system/files/chapters/c2144/c2144.pdf; https://github.com/davidamitchell/Research/blob/main/Research/completed/2026-03-10-nature-of-the-firm-coase-organisations.md] That asymmetry helps explain why markets for highly specific expertise rely heavily on hierarchy, repeated relationships, and reputation, rather than on one-shot spot verification.
