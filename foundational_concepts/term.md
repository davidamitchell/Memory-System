# Term

**Domain:** Knowledge representation and engineering

---

## Conceptual Definition

A term is a linguistic expression — a word or phrase — that has been assigned exactly one intended denotation within a defined domain, serving as the canonical label for a concept in that domain.

**Necessary conditions:**
1. **Linguistic** — it is a word or phrase in a natural or formal language.
2. **Domain-scoped** — its denotation is fixed within a specific domain; outside that domain it may denote differently or not at all.
3. **Univocal** — within its domain, it denotes exactly one concept (no polysemy within scope).
4. **Assigned** — the denotation is stipulated, not merely habitual; there is a definitional act binding term to concept.

**Sufficient conditions:**  
Any linguistic expression that has been stipulatively assigned a single denotation within a defined domain constitutes a term for that domain.

---

## Operational Definition

A term is operationally identified when:

1. It appears in a controlled vocabulary, glossary, or ontology for a domain.
2. A definition exists that states what the term denotes (necessary and sufficient conditions or explicit stipulation).
3. Any competent user of the domain can apply the term consistently to the same set of referents.
4. No other term in the same domain has the identical denotation (i.e., it is not a redundant synonym without declared equivalence).

---

## Distinguished From

| Term | Distinction |
|------|-------------|
| **Word** | A word is a unit of natural language with potentially multiple meanings; a term is a word or phrase given a single, domain-fixed meaning. |
| **Concept** | A concept is the abstract unit of meaning; a term is the linguistic label assigned to that concept. One concept may have multiple terms (synonyms) but each term denotes exactly one concept within the domain. |
| **Label** | A label is any identifier or name; a term additionally carries a formally defined denotation. |
