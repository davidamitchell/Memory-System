# Wisdom

**Domain:** Knowledge representation and engineering

---

## Conceptual Definition

Wisdom is the capacity to make sound, contextually appropriate judgments about when, whether, and how to apply knowledge in novel or complex situations, by integrating goals, values, consequences, and constraints that no rule or algorithm alone can fully capture.

**Necessary conditions:**
1. **Presupposes knowledge** — it can only be exercised by an agent that possesses relevant knowledge; wisdom without knowledge is uninformed judgment.
2. **Evaluative** — it involves assessing goals, values, and consequences, not merely deriving true conclusions.
3. **Context-sensitive** — the same knowledge may be applied differently in different situations; wisdom is the capacity to determine the appropriate application.
4. **Non-algorithmic** — it cannot be fully reduced to a procedure; it requires judgment in the face of incomplete specifications and trade-offs.

**Sufficient conditions:**  
Any capacity that integrates domain knowledge with evaluative judgment and context-sensitivity to produce sound decisions in novel or complex situations constitutes wisdom.

---

## Operational Definition

Wisdom is operationally evidenced when:

1. An agent correctly applies knowledge in situations where the applicable rules conflict, are incomplete, or have not been previously encountered.
2. The agent's decisions can be retrospectively justified by appeal to goals, values, and consequences — not merely to rules.
3. The agent knows when *not* to apply a piece of knowledge (recognising scope limits).
4. Outcomes produced by the agent are better (by agreed criteria) than those produced by rule-following alone.

---

## Distinguished From

| Term | Distinction |
|------|-------------|
| **Knowledge** | Knowledge is the content (verified facts and their relationships); wisdom is the capacity to judge its appropriate application. |
| **Insight** | An insight is a derived true statement; wisdom is the meta-capacity to judge the relevance and application of insights and knowledge. |
| **Intelligence** | Intelligence is the general capacity to learn and reason; wisdom specifically concerns evaluative, contextualised judgment in the application of knowledge. |
