# Theme

**Domain:** Knowledge representation and engineering

---

## Conceptual Definition

A theme is a recurrent, identifiable subject of concern that clusters semantically related concepts, terms, and documents within a domain, without itself constituting a formally defined class or concept with necessary and sufficient membership conditions.

**Necessary conditions:**
1. **Recurrent** — it appears across multiple documents or assertions; it is not singular or isolated.
2. **Identifiable** — it can be named and its scope described, even if its boundaries are approximate.
3. **Semantic** — it is defined by meaning-proximity, not by syntactic co-occurrence alone.
4. **Sub-formal** — it does not impose necessary and sufficient conditions on its members; membership is a matter of degree or judgment.

**Sufficient conditions:**  
Any recurrent, nameable, semantically coherent subject of concern that groups related concepts or documents without hard membership boundaries constitutes a theme.

---

## Operational Definition

A theme is operationally identified when:

1. A label can be assigned to it (e.g., "agentic-ai", "governance").
2. A set of documents or concepts can be listed as falling under it, with defensible reasons for inclusion.
3. Removing it from a classification system would reduce the cohesion of related groupings.
4. Its scope can be described in a sentence without enumerating every member.

---

## Distinguished From

| Term | Distinction |
|------|-------------|
| **Concept** | A concept has necessary and sufficient conditions; a theme has approximate, judgment-based boundaries. |
| **Class** | A class has formal membership criteria and is closed under a reasoner; a theme is open and approximate. |
| **Tag** | A tag is an arbitrary label assigned by an author; a theme is a shared, semantically grounded grouping whose validity is intersubjective. |
| **Topic** | Topic is an informal synonym; theme emphasises recurrence and semantic coherence across a corpus. |
